About

Why do evidence-based policies so often fail to take root? This question drives my research at the intersection of behavioral science, economics, and public policy.

I began my academic journey in economics and international relations, drawn to understanding how societies make decisions. Over time, I grew interested in the behavioral foundations of policymaking, particularly where traditional models fall short. My current work examines the economic, structural, and behavioral barriers that prevent rigorous evidence from being adopted or scaled, with attention to how historical and geographic contexts shape today’s policy challenges.

I explore these questions through both quantitative and qualitative approaches, with the goal of bridging the gap between research and practice. More broadly, I am committed to improving how governments and institutions incorporate interdisciplinary evidence into decision-making.

Outside of research, I enjoy watercolor painting, swimming, puzzles, gardening (both food and flowers), and spending time in nature.
